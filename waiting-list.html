Nobody Is Going To Tell You This

Here is something that is true and that almost nobody in your feed will say to you: the number going up is not the same thing as your life getting better.

I know that sounds obvious. It isn't. Because we have all, and I mean all, including people who would never admit it, started to confuse the two. The follower count and the life. The engagement rate and the sense of purpose. The reach and the belonging. We have let the metric become the meaning, and we did it so gradually that most of us didn't notice it happening.

I want to talk about why this happened, what it is doing to us, and what the alternative actually looks like. Not the alternative that gets posted about in the self-improvement corner of the internet, with the morning routines and the gratitude journals and the dopamine detox. A different alternative. One that is older than any of this and is about to become more important than it has been in a hundred years.

The number belongs to them.

Let's start with the thing nobody in the creator economy wants to say out loud.

Your followers do not belong to you. Your reach does not belong to you. The audience you spent years building, showing up every day, improving your craft, giving your attention and your time and your creative energy, belongs to the platform. Not to you. To them.

This is not a minor detail. This is the entire business model.

A ceramics teacher spends three years building an audience of fifty thousand people. She posts every day. She gets genuinely good. Not content-good, actually good, technically skilled, making things that take your breath away. And then the algorithm changes. Her reach drops seventy percent overnight. She starts paying for ads. She is now paying Instagram to reach the people who already chose to follow her. She created the value. They captured it.

This is not a bug. It is not an oversight. It is the design.

The design is: you build the audience, we own the audience, you pay us to access the audience you built. And you keep doing it because the alternative, starting over somewhere else, feels unthinkable after three years of work.

This is what a trap looks like when it is designed by very smart people with a lot of money.

The question worth asking is not "how do I grow faster?" The question is: what would I have if the platform disappeared tomorrow? The answer, for most people, is a number that lives on a server in California. Not relationships. Not trust. Not anything you could carry with you. A number.

You are not an influencer. You are a renter.

Power is the wrong word for what most people on these platforms have. What they have is tenancy. They are renting space in someone else's infrastructure, subject to the landlord's rules, which can change at any time without notice.

Real power, the kind that has actually mattered throughout human history, looks different. It looks like being known. Really known. By people who chose you not because an algorithm served them your content at the right moment, but because they have watched you work for years and they trust what your hands know.

A potter who is known by a hundred and fifty people, genuinely known, their name remembered, their process understood, their failures witnessed alongside their successes, has something that cannot be manufactured by any growth strategy. When she offers a workshop, it sells. Not because of reach. Because of trust that was built slowly, in a small place, where the relationships were real.

That is not less than a million followers. It is not a consolation prize for people who couldn't make it on the big platforms. It is a different thing entirely. It is the thing that has always mattered. We just forgot, because the number was so loud.

The wheel has an end.

I want to be honest with you about something that most people in the content creator space will not say because it is bad for their business to say it.

The economy is about to change in a way that makes the current hustle culture look like a brief, strange detour.

Artificial intelligence is not coming for the factory jobs. It is coming for the jobs that the people reading this are probably doing. Writing, designing, coding, marketing, researching, producing. The jobs that felt safe because they required creativity and intelligence. Those jobs. At a speed that has no historical precedent.

This is not doom. This is a transition. But transitions are only survivable if you know who you are before they happen.

The people who will be okay, not just economically okay but okay in the deeper sense of knowing what to do with their days and their hands and their time, are the people who built an identity that was never about the job. Who have a practice. Something they would keep doing if nobody was watching and nobody was paying. Something their hands know that takes years to learn and cannot be automated away because it lives in the body, not in the output.

The people who will struggle are the people whose entire identity is the metric. The follower count, the view count, the engagement rate, the salary. When those things change, and they are going to change, there is nothing underneath.

This is not a prediction about the distant future. This is happening now, to people you know, in industries you can name.

If the wheel stopped tomorrow, who would you be? Not what would you do. Who would you be.

The age problem nobody is talking about.

Here is something young people believe that is about to become the most expensive mistake of their generation: that age is a liability.

The impatience is real. The resentment is real. The sense that older people are in the way, taking up space, holding power that should have been handed over already. I understand where it comes from. The world has changed faster than institutions adapt. The people in charge grew up in a different reality and often cannot see this one clearly.

But there is a distinction that is being missed, and it is going to matter enormously in the world that is coming.

There is a difference between power that comes from position, from title, from seniority, from being in the room, and power that comes from knowledge accumulated over decades of doing something with your hands.

The first kind of power, young people are right to be impatient with. It is often structurally protected and often deserved less than the people holding it would like to believe.

The second kind of power is about to become more valuable than it has been in a hundred years.

Artificial intelligence is very good at doing things that can be described in steps. It is very good at tasks that have explicit rules. It is very good at producing outputs that resemble outputs it has seen before.

It is not good at the thing that takes forty years to build. The knowledge that lives in hands. The judgment that comes from having done something long enough to understand all the ways it can go wrong. The ability to read a situation that has no explicit rules because you have sat with enough situations to have developed something that functions like instinct.

A sixty-year-old blacksmith who has been working for forty years has something in his hands that no language model can replicate. Not because AI is stupid. Because that knowledge was built through time and repetition and failure and physical engagement with materials that resist and surprise and teach. It cannot be shortcut. It cannot be downloaded. It lives in the body and it took four decades to put it there.

Japan understood this. The Japanese government has a programme that officially designates craftspeople who carry knowledge so rare and so valuable that the state has a responsibility to protect it. Not their brand. Not their audience. Their knowledge. The thing in their hands. They are called Living National Treasures.

In the West, we call these people retired.

We have made age invisible in exactly the moment when age is about to become the most important currency there is. The knowledge that takes decades to build is the only knowledge that AI cannot replace. Young people who understand this will find the masters and learn from them. Young people who don't will watch their competitive advantages, speed, digital fluency, technical skill, be automated away while the people they dismissed as obsolete become irreplaceable.

And here is the thing already happening on the platforms: the older masters who finally learned to use Instagram and YouTube and TikTok are getting traction. Because they are simply better. A sixty-year-old ceramicist whose hands have been throwing for forty years posts a video and the craft is undeniable. Three years of content cannot compete with four decades of practice. The skill gap is visible in every frame. What they have cannot be faked and cannot be accelerated.

The acceleration and the settling.

The last hundred years moved fast. The next twenty are going to move faster. And then, this is the part nobody is saying, it will slow down again.

Every major transition in human history follows the same arc: acceleration, chaos, settling. The industrial revolution accelerated everything. There was enormous disruption. People lost their livelihoods, their communities, their sense of who they were. And then it settled. New rhythms emerged. New meaning was found. People built lives that worked in the new landscape.

We are in the acceleration phase right now. And the chaos is coming. But after the chaos, which will be shorter or longer depending on what people have built before it hits, there is a settling. A new pace. A return to depth over novelty.

And here is the thing about that settling: people who have been practising something for twenty years when it arrives are in a completely different position than people who spent those twenty years chasing metrics that no longer exist.

There is also a factor that almost nobody is talking about. The same artificial intelligence that is disrupting the labour economy is also transforming medicine and biology. The generation that survives this transition is likely to live longer than any generation before it. Not slightly longer. Significantly longer.

A sixty-year-old in 2040 might have more active years.

The question of what to do with a life is about to become a very long question. And the people who have been practising something, who have a community, a record, a thread of who they have been becoming over decades, will have a completely different relationship with that question than the people who built their identity on a platform and a number.

What it actually feels like.

I want to try to describe something that is hard to describe, because the vocabulary for it has been replaced by metrics.

There is a feeling that happens when you make something that is genuinely hard. Something that required you to try and fail and try again, that involved material or language or code or movement that resisted you and that you had to work to understand. And it comes out right. Or right enough.

It is not the same feeling as a post performing well. I know both feelings. They are not the same.

The performance metric gives you a hit of something, a validation, a confirmation that what you did was received. It is pleasant. It is also slightly hollow, in a way that becomes more apparent the more you have of it, not less. The dopamine loop is real but so is the diminishing return. More followers, and yet the feeling is the same or lesser. More engagement, and yet something is still missing.

The making feeling is different. It is not about reception. It is contact with the material, with your own capability, with the gap between what you imagined and what you actually managed to do. It tells you the truth. The clay doesn't lie. The code either runs or it doesn't. The piece either holds together or it cracks in the kiln.

This is the thing that the attention economy quietly took from a lot of people. Not overnight. Gradually, over years of optimising for reception rather than for making. The shift from "what am I trying to make?" to "what will perform?" is almost imperceptible at first. And then one day you realise you haven't made something for yourself in two years. Everything has been made for the number.

And the absence of that making feeling, the contact, the truth-telling resistance of material that doesn't care about your brand, is part of what sits underneath the restlessness. The sense that something is missing that you can't quite name, because the vocabulary for it has been optimised away.

It is not mindfulness. It is not self-care. It is not a morning routine.

It is making something that is genuinely hard. In a place where people know you well enough to understand what it cost. With someone watching who remembers what you said six months ago and is patient enough to wait for the right moment to ask about it.

The small place.

Human beings are not built for three billion. We are built for one hundred and fifty.

This is not a preference. This is biology. A researcher named Robin Dunbar spent years studying how many stable social relationships a human brain can maintain, how many people we can actually know, whose faces we can connect to names, whose situations we can track, with whom we have real mutual recognition. The answer is approximately one hundred and fifty. This number appears in hunter-gatherer bands, in Neolithic villages, in Roman military companies, in communities that have been studied across cultures and across centuries. It is a cognitive constant.

Every major social platform has been built in deliberate defiance of this number. Three billion users, not one hundred and fifty. The growth, always more growth, until the community becomes noise and the noise becomes the product and the product is your attention sold to people you will never meet.

What would a platform built around the number, around the actual limit of human relationship, look like?

It would look like a village. One hundred and fifty people maximum. When it fills, it seeds another. The growth of the network happens not by making the community bigger but by multiplying communities that stay human-sized. Each one with a founder who wrote the rules and meant them. Each one with a fire in the center where people talk without an algorithm deciding what surfaces. Each one with an economy that runs through the community rather than around it, where the teacher earns from the students who know her, not from the attention she generates for the platform.

And in each one, something watching. Not a manager. Not an algorithm optimising for engagement. A quiet presence, patient, almost never speaking, that reads what people write when nobody is performing, tracks where they go and where they avoid, notices when they go quiet, and occasionally, rarely, when the evidence has built into something worth saying, asks one question.

One question. From their own words. At the moment when the question belongs.

Then silence.

The question you have probably never been asked.

What would you do if the wheel stopped?

Not tomorrow. Not next week. If the whole game changed, the platform disappeared, the followers meant nothing, the content strategy was irrelevant, what would you do?

Most people don't have an answer. Or the answer they have is another metric, another platform, another strategy. The wheel again, just on different hardware.

The people who have an answer that isn't the wheel are the people who found something they would keep doing if nobody was watching. Who gave years to it. Who logged the failures and the slow improvements and the moments when something clicked that had been stuck for months. Who found a small group of people who were doing the same thing and sat with them, in the same place, long enough to actually know each other.

Those people have a thread. When the wheel stops, and it will stop, for everyone, in one form or another, they have something to hold onto. Not a number. A record. A practice. A place. People who remember who they were before it all started moving so fast.

I am building that place. Not for everyone. For the person who suspects, somewhere underneath the scroll, that there is something more real than the number going up.

The fire is warm. Sit for a while.

I am building this. It is not finished yet. When it is ready for the first people, I will send one email. Not a newsletter. Not a sequence. Just the moment when the door opens.

One email when it opens. Nothing else.
