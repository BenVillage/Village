the cook together philosophy

Two roles. One kitchen. Shared moments.

Cook Together is not just about food. It's about teamwork, connection, and the joy of preparing something delicious side by side.

YOU
The Line Cook

YOU manages all the heat. The stove, the oven, the grill. YOU controls the timing — when things go in, when they come out, how long they rest. YOU finishes the dish.

Every YOU step is written for someone who's at the stove, focused on the cooking process. When ME has something ready, YOU reach for it — it will be there.

"YOU is the conductor. ME plays every instrument."
ME
The Prep Chef

ME handles everything before it reaches the heat. Chopping, peeling, measuring, marinating. ME prepares each component and passes it to YOU at exactly the right moment.

ME's job is to make YOU look brilliant. Everything prepped, everything ready. When a YOU step says "Add the garlic from ME" — ME has it in their hand already.

"ME is the reason YOU can cook without chaos."
Us
The Shared Finish

Every recipe ends with an Us step — the final moment you do together. The toss. The plate. The pour. It's brief, and it's the best part.

Before You Begin

Great cooking together starts before the first chop. Shop smart, set up your kitchen as a shared space, and protect your devices.

Choose a recipe and we'll build your complete shopping list — split by who needs what. Tap each item to check it off as you go. Shop together or divide and conquer.

Cooking together is a bonding experience — and it flows best when the kitchen is set up for two. A few minutes now makes the whole evening smoother.

i

Two zones, one kitchen

Give each person their own counter space before you start. ME on the prep side, YOU by the stove. Define your territory — it turns chaos into choreography.

ii

One board and knife each

ME will be chopping constantly. Give ME their own cutting board and knife so YOU never has to wait. This single thing halves your cooking time.

iii

ME's mise en place

ME: line up every ingredient before cooking begins. Small bowls for prepped items. When YOU calls for something, it should be in your hand, not on the shelf.

iv

YOU: know your stove

Before ME starts chopping, YOU should confirm pans, check heat sources, and know the recipe's timing. YOU is the conductor — start thinking like one.

v

Wash hands first — both of you

Obvious. Worth saying. Especially ME, who'll be touching everything and passing it directly to YOU and the pan.

vi

Clear the sink

You'll need it during cooking. Clear it now, put a dish towel over one side. Less friction mid-recipe means more time enjoying the process.

vii

Temper your proteins

If the recipe has meat or fish, take it out of the fridge 15–20 minutes before YOU needs it. Cold proteins in a hot pan cook unevenly. ME handles this.

viii

Read through together first

Both of you. The whole recipe. Even two minutes of shared reading at the start prevents ten minutes of mid-cook confusion. This is part of the experience.

Keeping your screen safe in the kitchen

1
Use a phone stand or cookbook holder. Never lay your phone flat on the counter near the stove. Steam and oil splatter are the enemies. Prop it up at eye level, behind your prep area.
2
Keep the laptop on a side table or shelf. Never on the main counter where chopping happens. A wooden kitchen shelf or a bar stool beside the counter is ideal.
3
Dry your hands before touching the screen. Make it a habit — wipe on your apron before you tap. Water damage is slow and invisible until it isn't.
4
Watch the steam. Boiling pots and the oven door produce bursts of steam that can fog and damage screens. Keep devices at least 60cm away from active heat.
5
Use a screen protector. A tempered glass protector on your phone is cheap. Oil splatter wipes off glass; it stays on naked screens.
6
The 45 degree rule for tablets. An iPad in a case standing at 45 degrees on a cookbook stand is the perfect kitchen companion. Far enough back, readable at distance, easy to swipe with a knuckle.
7
Raise your screen brightness. Kitchen lights and steam make screens hard to read. Max brightness, and consider bumping your text size in accessibility settings.
8
Disable auto-lock. Nothing worse than a screen locking mid-step when your hands are covered in flour. Set screen timeout to 5 minutes while cooking.
Pro tip — the shared screen setup Put the laptop on a small shelf or stack of cookbooks at the back of the counter, slightly raised. Both of you can glance up and read it. Phones stay in your pockets or in a charging holder on the wall. This is the move.
